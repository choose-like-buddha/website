Someone you love is hurting, and you can't fix it. You'd take their pain into your own body if you could. Instead you hover, helpless, offering solutions they don't want and reassurances that ring hollow. The wish to make it better is overwhelming, and the inability to do so is its own kind of agony. You feel that you're failing them simply by being unable to lift the weight.

## The Mindful Approach

The deepest instinct of love is to remove a loved one's pain — and one of love's hardest lessons is that we usually can't. We cannot live their experience or carry their grief for them. But we can offer something that matters even more than a solution: steady, non-anxious presence, a witness who stays. To accompany is not to fix, and accompaniment is often what's truly needed.

- **Offer presence before advice.** The urge to fix can crowd out the person's actual need, which is usually just to be heard and not left alone. Listen more than you counsel. "I'm here, and I'm not going anywhere" often heals more than any solution you could propose.
- **Don't take their pain as yours to solve.** Carrying their suffering as if it were your responsibility helps no one and depletes you. You can be deeply moved by their pain while still recognizing that the path through it is theirs to walk. Your job is to walk beside, not to walk it for them.
- **Tend to yourself so you can keep showing up.** Supporting a suffering loved one is draining, and a depleted caregiver soon has nothing left to give. Resting, grieving your own helplessness, and accepting your limits aren't selfish — they're what allow you to stay present over the long haul.

## A Practice for Today

Reach out to your loved one today, but lead with presence rather than solutions. Say simply, "I'm here, and you don't have to go through this alone." Resist the urge to fix; instead, listen, and let your steadiness be the gift. Afterward, acknowledge your own helplessness with compassion — you are doing the hardest and most loving thing, which is to stay. You cannot remove their pain, but you can make sure they never face it by themselves.
