You've explained, pleaded, modeled, waited. You can see exactly how their life — and yours with them — could be better, if only they'd shift. But they don't. The same patterns repeat, the same promises dissolve, and you're left exhausted by the gap between who they are and who you keep hoping they'll become. Your love has quietly turned into a renovation project, and the building refuses to be renovated.

## The Mindful Approach

You cannot change another person. You can invite, influence, and inspire, but the decision to change belongs to them alone — and often arrives, if it ever does, on a timeline and through a door you don't control. Much of your suffering lives in the refusal to accept this. Peace begins when you stop staking your wellbeing on someone else's transformation.

- **Release the project of fixing them.** Treating someone as a problem to be solved strains the relationship and exhausts you, while rarely producing change — people resist being renovated. Loving someone as they are, rather than as your improved version of them, frees you both.
- **Accept that change must be self-chosen.** No amount of your effort can install motivation in another person. They will change when their own pain or longing makes it worth it to them, not when your frustration peaks. Pushing harder often only entrenches their resistance.
- **Decide what you'll do, since you can't decide what they'll do.** The real question shifts from "how do I change them?" to "given who they actually are, how do I want to live?" That may mean adjusting expectations, setting boundaries, or accepting them fully. The agency that was never over them is entirely over you.

## A Practice for Today

Think of the person you've been trying to change. Silently grant them their freedom: "It's not mine to make you different. You get to choose." Notice the relief, and the grief, in releasing the project. Then turn the question inward: given who they are right now, how do I want to respond, protect myself, or love them honestly? You'll find your power was never in their change — it was always in your own response.
