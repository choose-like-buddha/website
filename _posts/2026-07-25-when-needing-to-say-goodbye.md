The end has come, or is coming — to a relationship, a chapter, a person, a place. You stand at the threshold knowing that after this, things will not be the same. There's a heaviness in the leaving, a pull to hold on just a little longer, to delay the moment when it becomes the past. Saying goodbye asks you to release what you weren't finished loving, and everything in you wants to refuse.

## The Mindful Approach

Impermanence is the nature of everything; all that begins must one day end. This isn't a cruelty but the very condition that makes things precious. Saying goodbye well means honoring what was, feeling the grief that love leaves behind, and gradually opening your hands — not because it didn't matter, but because clinging only adds suffering to the sorrow.

- **Let grief be the measure of the love.** The pain of goodbye is proportional to the depth of what you're leaving. Don't rush to numb or minimize it. Grief is not a problem to solve but love continuing in a new form — the heart's honest response to having cared.
- **Honor what was before releasing it.** A goodbye deserves acknowledgment. Take time to recognize what this person, place, or chapter gave you, what it taught you, what you'll carry forward. Gratitude doesn't erase the loss, but it lets the ending hold meaning rather than just emptiness.
- **Open your hands without erasing the past.** Letting go doesn't mean forgetting or pretending it didn't matter. It means loosening your grip so you can carry the love without being trapped in the loss. What was real remains real, even as you walk on.

## A Practice for Today

If a goodbye is before you, take a quiet moment to mark it. Name aloud or in writing what this chapter gave you, and what you're grateful for. Let yourself feel the grief without apology — it's the truest tribute you can offer. Then, gently, speak the goodbye: "Thank you. I'm letting go now." You carry forward everything that mattered. Releasing the form is how you keep the meaning.
