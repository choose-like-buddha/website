You love them, and they hurt you. Not always, not entirely — that's what makes it so hard. The good moments are real, which keeps you hoping, explaining, forgiving. But the pain is real too, and it keeps returning. You're caught between the love you feel and the harm you're absorbing, telling yourself that if you just love better, the hurting will stop. It rarely does.

## The Mindful Approach

Love and harm can coexist, and that's one of the most confusing truths of the heart. Caring for someone does not obligate you to endure mistreatment, and tolerating harm is not a higher form of love — it's often a slow erasure of yourself. Real love includes love for your own wellbeing, and sometimes the most loving response is a firm boundary, or even distance.

- **Don't let love become a reason to accept harm.** "But I love them" is not a reason to keep absorbing damage. Love and self-respect are meant to travel together. When love demands that you abandon yourself, something has gone wrong that more devotion won't fix.
- **Separate their pain from your responsibility.** People who hurt others are often hurting themselves, and compassion for that is valid. But understanding the source of harm doesn't mean you must remain its target. You can have empathy for someone and still protect yourself from them.
- **Recognize that boundaries can be an act of love.** Allowing someone to keep harming you isn't good for them either — it enables a pattern that damages you both. A clear limit, even a painful distance, can be the most honest and caring response available. Love sometimes looks like "no."

## A Practice for Today

Reflect honestly on a relationship where love and hurt are entangled. Ask: "Am I confusing endurance with love?" Then identify one boundary that would protect your wellbeing — a limit named, a pattern refused, a difficult conversation begun. You can hold compassion for them in one hand and your own safety in the other. Loving someone has never required you to keep standing in the path of their harm.
