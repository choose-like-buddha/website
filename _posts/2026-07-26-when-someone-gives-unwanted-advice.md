You didn't ask, but here it comes — how you should live, parent, work, heal, decide. They mean well, perhaps, but the advice lands like a small judgment, an implication that you haven't thought it through yourself. You feel the irritation rise, the urge to defend, the temptation to snap back. And whether you argue or just nod stiffly, you're left vaguely diminished, as though your own competence were in question.

## The Mindful Approach

Unsolicited advice is usually more about the giver than the receiver — their need to feel helpful, their anxiety, their own unresolved choices projected onto your situation. Understanding this frees you from taking it as an insult or an instruction. You can let the advice pass through without absorbing the judgment, and without surrendering your authority over your own life.

- **Hear the intention beneath the words.** Much unwanted advice comes wrapped in clumsy care. The person may simply be expressing love, worry, or the wish to connect, in the only language they know. Receiving the intention kindly, even while setting the content aside, keeps the peace and your dignity both.
- **Remember you remain the author of your life.** Advice is input, not a command. You can listen, thank them, and still do exactly as you judged best. Their opinion does not obligate you, and your choices need no one's ratification. The pen stays in your hand.
- **Respond with grace, not defensiveness.** Arguing or justifying yourself often invites more advice and more friction. A simple "Thank you, I'll think about that" honors them without committing you to anything. You can be gracious without being governed.

## A Practice for Today

The next time unwanted advice arrives, pause before reacting. Look for the care beneath it, however awkwardly expressed, and respond with a calm "Thanks — I'll consider it." Then quietly remind yourself: this is their view, not your verdict, and the choice remains entirely yours. You don't have to argue, defend, or comply. You can simply let the advice pass by like weather, and keep walking your own road.
