They still try to run your life — the opinions on your choices, the disapproval when you deviate, the guilt deployed when you assert yourself. No matter your age, a part of you shrinks back into the child seeking approval, and another part bristles at being managed. You love them, and you resent them, and you're tired of the tug-of-war over a life that is supposed to be yours.

## The Mindful Approach

Controlling parents often act from anxiety dressed as love — a fear for your wellbeing that expresses itself as the need to direct you. This doesn't make the control acceptable, but it helps you respond with compassion rather than only anger. The task of adulthood is to claim authority over your own life — firmly, respectfully, and without waiting for permission that may never come.

- **See the fear beneath the control.** Most controlling behavior is driven by worry — the parent's discomfort with uncertainty, projected onto you. Recognizing the anxiety behind the intrusion lets you stay compassionate even as you decline to be governed by it. You can love the worrier without obeying the worry.
- **Set boundaries without waiting for approval.** You will likely never get their blessing to live your own way — and you don't need it. Boundaries are stated, not negotiated: "I've decided this, and I hope you can respect it." Their disapproval is survivable; the slow surrender of your own life is not.
- **Release the child's hunger for permission.** Part of what keeps you entangled is the lingering wish for parental approval. Becoming your own authority means accepting that you may have to act without it. You can honor your parent and still live a life they wouldn't have chosen for you.

## A Practice for Today

Identify one area where a parent's control still shapes your choices. Acknowledge the fear that likely drives it — "they're scared for me" — so you can respond with compassion rather than war. Then state one calm boundary, without seeking their approval: "This is my decision." Notice the discomfort of not being blessed, and let it be. Honoring your parent does not require handing them the pen to your life.
