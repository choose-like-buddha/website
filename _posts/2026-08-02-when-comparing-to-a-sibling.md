They got the success, the praise, the path that seemed to come easily. You measure yourself against them and the old childhood ranking flares up again — who achieved more, who made the family prouder, who turned out better. Even as adults, the comparison runs underneath everything. Their wins can sting, and you hate that they sting, and the whole thing keeps you from simply living your own life on its own terms.

## The Mindful Approach

Sibling comparison is among the oldest and stickiest, rooted in childhoods spent competing for the same attention and approval. But the premise is false: you and your sibling are not running the same race, and were never meant to. Each life has its own terrain, timing, and meaning. Comparison measures your insides against their outsides and guarantees that you'll lose sight of your own road.

- **See that you're comparing different lives, not ranking the same one.** Your sibling's path has different conditions, values, and hidden struggles than yours. Holding your life against theirs is like comparing two entirely different journeys and calling one a failure. There's no shared scoreboard, however much childhood implied there was.
- **Notice the family story you've absorbed.** Often the comparison isn't even yours — it's a ranking the family assigned long ago that you've kept enforcing on yourself. Question whether the measuring stick is one you actually believe in, or just one you inherited and never put down.
- **Define success by your own values.** When you let someone else's life be the standard, you abandon your own definition of a life well lived. Return to what genuinely matters to you. Measured against your own values rather than your sibling's highlights, your life looks entirely different.

## A Practice for Today

Notice one way you've been comparing yourself to a sibling. Ask honestly: "Is this even my measure of a good life, or one I inherited?" Then name one thing in your own life that aligns with what you genuinely value — not what would win the family contest. Let that be the measure today. Your sibling is walking their road; you are walking yours. There was never a finish line you were both meant to cross.
