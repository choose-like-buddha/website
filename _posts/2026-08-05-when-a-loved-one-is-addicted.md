You watch someone you love disappear into something that's destroying them. You've pleaded, helped, threatened, rescued, researched — and still the addiction holds. You oscillate between hope and despair, between enabling and ultimatums, between rage and grief. Their suffering becomes your suffering, their crisis your full-time worry, and somewhere in the chaos you've begun to lose the outline of your own life.

## The Mindful Approach

Loving someone in active addiction is one of the most painful experiences a person can endure, in part because love makes us want to save them — and we cannot. Recovery is something only they can choose, on a timeline you don't control. The hard, freeing truth is that your wellbeing cannot be held hostage to their choices, and that detaching with love is sometimes the most loving thing of all.

- **Accept the limits of your power.** You did not cause their addiction, you cannot control it, and you cannot cure it. Holding yourself responsible for someone else's recovery is a weight that crushes you while changing nothing. Releasing what was never yours to carry is not abandonment; it's reality.
- **Recognize when helping becomes enabling.** Love can blur into rescuing that shields them from the consequences they may need to feel. Ask honestly whether your help is supporting recovery or cushioning the addiction. Sometimes the most caring act is to stop softening the fall.
- **Protect your own life and seek support.** You cannot pour endlessly into someone else's crisis without being drained dry. Tending to your own wellbeing — through boundaries, your own support, time for your own life — is not selfish. It's what lets you survive this, and remain someone they could return to.

## A Practice for Today

Take a moment to acknowledge the limits of your power with compassion: "I cannot control this, and it is not mine to cure." Identify one boundary that protects your own wellbeing — a limit on rescuing, a space you reclaim for your own life, a conversation with someone who supports you. Loving an addicted person does not require you to drown alongside them. You can keep your love and your life both.
